.. _choosing-algorithm:

==========================================
Choosing an Algorithm
==========================================

QC Lab ships with four Algorithms, organized into two cross-compatible
families. The choice of Algorithm is constrained by the basis in which
the Model object is defined and by the physics being studied. This
section provides a decision tree, a compatibility matrix, and short
notes on when mean-field dynamics and fewest-switches surface hopping
(FSSH) give meaningfully different predictions.

For the Algorithm objects' source-level documentation, see
:ref:`Algorithms <algorithm>`. For the *ab initio* workflow, see
:ref:`Ab Initio Dynamics <ab-initio>`.

----

The four built-in Algorithms
============================

.. list-table::
   :header-rows: 1
   :widths: 32 18 50

   * - Algorithm
     - Basis
     - Class
   * - Mean-field dynamics (also known as Ehrenfest dynamics)
     - diabatic
     - :class:`qclab.algorithms.MeanField`
   * - Mean-field dynamics, *ab initio*
     - adiabatic
     - :class:`qclab.algorithms.MeanFieldAbInitio`
   * - Fewest-switches surface hopping (FSSH)
     - diabatic
     - :class:`qclab.algorithms.FewestSwitchesSurfaceHopping`
   * - Fewest-switches surface hopping, *ab initio*
     - adiabatic
     - :class:`qclab.algorithms.FewestSwitchesSurfaceHoppingAbInitio`

The two columns of the table mirror two design choices. The basis
column matters for compatibility with the Model object: a diabatic
Algorithm pairs only with a Model defined in a diabatic basis, and
an adiabatic Algorithm pairs only with an *ab initio* Model. The
class column matters for which Tasks the Algorithm uses internally;
the mean-field Algorithms propagate a single mean-field wavefunction,
the FSSH Algorithms propagate a wavefunction together with an active
surface.

The compatibility rule is mechanical and has no exceptions: any
diabatic Algorithm runs against any diabatic Model; the
:class:`qclab.models.AbInitio` Model pairs only with the two
``*AbInitio`` Algorithms. Mixing the two families is not supported
because the underlying Tasks expect different ingredients to be
present (``derivative_coupling_dzc`` for adiabatic-basis work,
``h_qc`` and ``dh_qc_dzc`` for diabatic-basis work).

----

Decision tree
=============

Start at the Model and follow the questions:

#. **Is the system described by a global diabatic Hamiltonian?**

   - **Yes** — pick one of the diabatic Algorithms,
     :class:`qclab.algorithms.MeanField` or
     :class:`qclab.algorithms.FewestSwitchesSurfaceHopping`, and
     proceed to step 2. Most of the built-in Models live here.
   - **No** — the Hamiltonian is produced on the fly by an
     electronic-structure code. Pair the
     :class:`qclab.models.AbInitio` Model with either
     :class:`qclab.algorithms.MeanFieldAbInitio` or
     :class:`qclab.algorithms.FewestSwitchesSurfaceHoppingAbInitio`,
     and proceed to step 2.

#. **What kind of population transfer matters?**

   - **Smooth, weakly nonadiabatic, or in a strongly coupled
     regime where multiple surfaces contribute throughout** — pick
     mean-field. The mean-field wavefunction averages over surfaces
     coherently and is appropriate when the population transfer is
     a smooth function of time.
   - **Sharply nonadiabatic, with discrete population transfer
     events** — pick FSSH. The active-surface picture captures the
     discrete branching that mean-field washes out.

A pragmatic workflow is to run both Algorithms on the same Model and
compare; the two often agree on early-time dynamics and disagree on
the long-time branching ratios. The disagreement is informative.

----

Mean-field vs. FSSH
===================

Mean-field dynamics, also known as Ehrenfest dynamics, propagates a
single classical trajectory in the average potential generated by
the quantum wavefunction. The classical force is

.. math::

    F_\xi = -\langle \psi | \frac{\partial \hat{H}_\mathrm{q\text{-}c}}{\partial q_\xi} | \psi \rangle,

so the dynamics on every trajectory feels a mean field weighted by
the current populations. The strength of the approach is its
simplicity and the smoothness of the resulting trajectories; its
weakness is that the dynamics never branches — long after a region
of strong nonadiabatic coupling, the classical coordinate continues
to evolve in a state-averaged potential that no real chemical
process resembles.

Fewest-switches surface hopping (FSSH) keeps the same mean-field
electronic propagation but evolves the classical coordinate on a
single adiabatic surface at a time. At every update time step, a
stochastic test compares a hopping probability to a random number
and either keeps the trajectory on the active surface or hops to a
neighboring one. Trajectory branching emerges from many independent
stochastic histories.

For a fixed Model, the two Algorithms answer different questions.
Mean-field is the right choice when the observable of interest is a
state-averaged property and when surface-hopping artifacts (forbidden
hops, decoherence-induced overcoherence) would dominate the
disagreement with reality. FSSH is the right choice when discrete
branching is part of the physics — photoexcited dynamics that have
to relax onto specific product surfaces, vibronic models near
conical intersections, transport in tight-binding models with
band-edge populations.

The QC Lab paper (Krotz et al., *J. Chem. Theory Comput.* **2026**,
*22*, 3144–3152) reports benchmarks of both Algorithms against
exact quantum dynamics for the spin-boson and Holstein lattice
models; users designing new benchmarks may find those reference
data useful.

Choosing settings
=================

Once the Algorithm class is chosen, three Constants need attention:

``sim.settings.dt_update``
    The integrator time step. RK4 in QC Lab is stable for
    ``dt_update`` up to roughly the inverse of the largest
    frequency in the Hamiltonian; if the integrator destabilizes,
    halve ``dt_update``.

``sim.settings.dt_collect``
    The collect time step. Should be a multiple of ``dt_update``.
    Coarser ``dt_collect`` saves disk space for long runs but
    sub-samples the recorded dynamics; pick ``dt_collect`` such
    that the resolved oscillations are not aliased.

``sim.settings.num_trajs``
    The Monte Carlo sample size. Mean-field results converge with
    a few hundred trajectories for most diabatic Models; FSSH
    typically needs thousands for population error bars at the
    percent level. The exact number depends on the Model and the
    observable.

Algorithm-specific settings live on the Algorithm object's Constants
object; see :ref:`The Constants Object <constants>`. The two most
common are ``fssh_deterministic`` (which fixes hop draws to a
deterministic schedule for unit testing) and ``gauge_fixing`` (which
selects the gauge-fixing scheme for adiabatic-basis algorithms).
