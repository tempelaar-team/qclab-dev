Choosing an Algorithm#

QC Lab ships with four Algorithms, organized into two cross-compatible families. The choice of Algorithm is constrained by the basis in which the Model object is defined and by the physics being studied. This section provides a decision tree, a compatibility matrix, and short notes on when mean-field dynamics and fewest-switches surface hopping (FSSH) give meaningfully different predictions.

For the Algorithm objects’ source-level documentation, see Algorithms. For the ab initio workflow, see Ab Initio Dynamics.

The two columns of the table mirror two design choices. The basis column matters for compatibility with the Model object: a diabatic Algorithm pairs only with a Model defined in a diabatic basis, and an adiabatic Algorithm pairs only with an ab initio Model. The class column matters for which Tasks the Algorithm uses internally; the mean-field Algorithms propagate a single mean-field wavefunction, the FSSH Algorithms propagate a wavefunction together with an active surface.

The compatibility rule is mechanical and has no exceptions: any diabatic Algorithm runs against any diabatic Model; the qclab.models.AbInitio Model pairs only with the two *AbInitio Algorithms. Mixing the two families is not supported because the underlying Tasks expect different ingredients to be present (derivative_coupling_dzc for adiabatic-basis work, h_qc and dh_qc_dzc for diabatic-basis work).

Decision tree#

Start at the Model and follow the questions:

  1. Is the system described by a global diabatic Hamiltonian?

    • Yes — pick one of the diabatic Algorithms, qclab.algorithms.MeanField or qclab.algorithms.FewestSwitchesSurfaceHopping, and proceed to step 2. Most of the built-in Models live here.

    • No — the Hamiltonian is produced on the fly by an electronic-structure code. Pair the qclab.models.AbInitio Model with either qclab.algorithms.MeanFieldAbInitio or qclab.algorithms.FewestSwitchesSurfaceHoppingAbInitio, and proceed to step 2.

  2. What kind of population transfer matters?

    • Smooth, weakly nonadiabatic, or in a strongly coupled regime where multiple surfaces contribute throughout — pick mean-field. The mean-field wavefunction averages over surfaces coherently and is appropriate when the population transfer is a smooth function of time.

    • Sharply nonadiabatic, with discrete population transfer events — pick FSSH. The active-surface picture captures the discrete branching that mean-field washes out.

A pragmatic workflow is to run both Algorithms on the same Model and compare; the two often agree on early-time dynamics and disagree on the long-time branching ratios. The disagreement is informative.

Mean-field vs. FSSH#

Mean-field dynamics, also known as Ehrenfest dynamics, propagates a single classical trajectory in the average potential generated by the quantum wavefunction. The classical force is

\[F_\xi = -\langle \psi | \frac{\partial \hat{H}_\mathrm{q\text{-}c}}{\partial q_\xi} | \psi \rangle,\]

so the dynamics on every trajectory feels a mean field weighted by the current populations. The strength of the approach is its simplicity and the smoothness of the resulting trajectories; its weakness is that the dynamics never branches — long after a region of strong nonadiabatic coupling, the classical coordinate continues to evolve in a state-averaged potential that no real chemical process resembles.

Fewest-switches surface hopping (FSSH) keeps the same mean-field electronic propagation but evolves the classical coordinate on a single adiabatic surface at a time. At every update time step, a stochastic test compares a hopping probability to a random number and either keeps the trajectory on the active surface or hops to a neighboring one. Trajectory branching emerges from many independent stochastic histories.

For a fixed Model, the two Algorithms answer different questions. Mean-field is the right choice when the observable of interest is a state-averaged property and when surface-hopping artifacts (forbidden hops, decoherence-induced overcoherence) would dominate the disagreement with reality. FSSH is the right choice when discrete branching is part of the physics — photoexcited dynamics that have to relax onto specific product surfaces, vibronic models near conical intersections, transport in tight-binding models with band-edge populations.

The QC Lab paper (Krotz et al., J. Chem. Theory Comput. 2026, 22, 3144–3152) reports benchmarks of both Algorithms against exact quantum dynamics for the spin-boson and Holstein lattice models; users designing new benchmarks may find those reference data useful.

Choosing settings#

Once the Algorithm class is chosen, three Constants need attention:

sim.settings.dt_update

The integrator time step. RK4 in QC Lab is stable for dt_update up to roughly the inverse of the largest frequency in the Hamiltonian; if the integrator destabilizes, halve dt_update.

sim.settings.dt_collect

The collect time step. Should be a multiple of dt_update. Coarser dt_collect saves disk space for long runs but sub-samples the recorded dynamics; pick dt_collect such that the resolved oscillations are not aliased.

sim.settings.num_trajs

The Monte Carlo sample size. Mean-field results converge with a few hundred trajectories for most diabatic Models; FSSH typically needs thousands for population error bars at the percent level. The exact number depends on the Model and the observable.

Algorithm-specific settings live on the Algorithm object’s Constants object; see The Constants Object. The two most common are fssh_deterministic (which fixes hop draws to a deterministic schedule for unit testing) and gauge_fixing (which selects the gauge-fixing scheme for adiabatic-basis algorithms).

Adding a new Algorithm — a new propagator, a new surface-hopping variant, a different decoherence correction — is covered in Developing Custom Algorithms. The checklist there pairs with the Recipes section.
